Q: Distinguish between individual demand curve and market demand curve?

Definition:

Individual Demand Curve: Represents the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded by a single consumer.

Market Demand Curve: Represents the relationship between the price of a good and the total quantity demanded by all consumers in the market.

Scope:

Individual Demand Curve: Limited to a single consumer.

Market Demand Curve: Encompasses all consumers in the market, giving a comprehensive picture of overall demand.

Construction:

Individual Demand Curve: Directly shows one individual's willingness and ability to purchase at different prices.

Market Demand Curve: Obtained by horizontally summing up all individual demand curves (i.e., adding the quantities demanded by all consumers at each price level).

Graphical Representation:

Individual Demand Curve: A single curve depicting one person's demand behavior.

Market Demand Curve: A cumulative curve showing the demand behavior of the entire market.

Determinants:

Individual Demand Curve: Influenced by personal income, taste, preferences, and price of the good.

Market Demand Curve: Influenced by the same factors collectively, as well as the size and composition of the population.

Relevance:

Individual Demand Curve: Useful for studying microeconomic behavior of a single consumer.

Market Demand Curve: Important for analyzing market trends, setting prices, and formulating economic policies.

Shape: Both curves usually slope downward from left to right, indicating the law of demand.

Focus:

Individual Demand Curve: Focuses on individual consumer behavior.

Market Demand Curve: Provides an aggregate picture of the market demand at various price levels.

Q: Discuss the factors affecting the price elasticity of demand?

Nature of the Good:

Necessities like food and medicine have inelastic demand as they are essential. Luxuries like jewelry or designer clothing have elastic demand because their purchase can be postponed.

Availability of Substitutes:

If close substitutes are available, demand tends to be elastic because consumers can switch to alternatives when the price increases. If substitutes are limited, demand becomes inelastic.

Proportion of Income Spent:

Goods that take up a large portion of income, like cars, have elastic demand, while inexpensive goods like salt have inelastic demand.

Time Period:

In the short term, demand is usually inelastic as consumers need time to adjust their habits. Over the long term, demand becomes more elastic.

Habitual Consumption:

Goods that are habit-forming, like tobacco or alcohol, have inelastic demand because consumers continue buying despite price changes.

Definition of the Market:

Broadly defined markets (e.g., "food") tend to have inelastic demand, while narrowly defined markets (e.g., "organic apples") have more elastic demand due to specific substitutes.

Durability of the Good:

Durable goods (e.g., cars, appliances) tend to have elastic demand because their purchase can be postponed. Perishable goods are more inelastic.

Advertising and Brand Loyalty:

Strong advertising and brand loyalty make demand inelastic, as consumers stick to a particular brand regardless of price changes.

Q: Discuss the exceptions of the law of demand?

Giffen Goods: These are inferior goods whose demand increases when their price rises because consumers cut back on more expensive substitutes.

Example: Poor families buying more potatoes when prices rise as they can't afford alternatives.

Veblen Goods (Conspicuous Consumption): Luxury goods become more desirable when their prices increase.

Example: Designer brands, luxury watches, high-end cars.

Necessities: Some essential goods have inelastic demand, meaning their demand doesn’t change much with price.

Example: Life-saving medicines like insulin, basic utilities like electricity.

Speculation and Future Expectations: People may buy more if they expect prices to rise further.

Example: Investors buying more real estate despite rising prices, expecting future value appreciation.

Bandwagon Effect: People buy goods just because others are buying them, regardless of price.

Example: Trending fashion items, viral products.

Ignorance Effect: Consumers may assume that a higher price means better quality.

Example: Expensive health supplements perceived as more effective.

Habitual Consumption: Some goods are consumed regularly, even when prices rise.

Example: Tobacco, alcohol, coffee.

Q: Differentiate between the Law of Variable Proportion and the Law of Returns to Scale?

1. Definition:

Law of Variable Proportion: Examines the relationship between input and output when one input (e.g., labor) is variable while others (e.g., capital) are fixed. It operates in the short run where at least one factor remains constant.

Law of Returns to Scale: Explores the relationship between input and output when all inputs are increased proportionately. It operates in the long run where all factors of production are variable.

2. Time Frame:

Variable Proportion: Applies to the short run.

Returns to Scale: Applies to the long run.

3. Nature of Inputs:

Variable Proportion: Varies only one factor of production (e.g., labor) while keeping others constant.

Returns to Scale: Changes all factors of production simultaneously and in the same proportion.

4. Stages:

Variable Proportion: Divided into three stages:

  • Increasing Returns
  • Diminishing Returns
  • Negative Returns

Returns to Scale: Divided into three types:

  • Increasing Returns to Scale (output increases more than inputs)
  • Constant Returns to Scale (output increases in the same proportion as inputs)
  • Decreasing Returns to Scale (output increases less than inputs)

5. Scale of Operation:

Variable Proportion: Deals with small-scale changes by adjusting a single input.

Returns to Scale: Deals with large-scale changes in production by proportionately varying all inputs.

6. Practical Application:

Variable Proportion: Helps understand short-term production issues, e.g., agricultural productivity by adding more labor to fixed land.

Returns to Scale: Useful in analyzing long-term growth strategies, e.g., expanding a factory by doubling labor, machinery, and raw materials.

7. Assumptions:

Variable Proportion: Assumes one factor is fixed.

Returns to Scale: Assumes all factors can be varied.

8. Example:

Variable Proportion: Adding more workers to a fixed amount of land—initially, output increases, then diminishes, and eventually turns negative.

Returns to Scale: Doubling both workers and machinery in a factory—output may increase more than double, exactly double, or less than double.
